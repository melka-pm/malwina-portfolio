How I built an AI operating system for a recovery project that had nothing

I'm a project manager who was handed a revived healthcare project with a vague scope, no business analyst, and no company AI workflows. Instead of drowning, I built an AI-powered operating system that ran my documentation, reporting, and requirements pipeline. This is how, and what I learned. And if this is what I can do in worst-case conditions, imagine what I can do in normal ones.

houston, we have

THE SITUATION

A UK client was building a platform connecting carers, doctors, and nurses. The project had been paused for almost a year. When it came back, the previous project manager was gone. I inherited: four developers (2 backend, 2 frontend), a designer, a vague and incomplete scope, no business analyst, no onboarding, a client with very limited availability, and stakeholders who had lost confidence in the project and held no hard decision-making power. On top of that, my company had no established AI workflows and was mid-migration between project management tools.

My job: deliver anyway.

WHAT I BUILT

Stack: Claude (paid plan, Projects + connectors), Read.ai, Slack, Gmail. No engineering support, no extra budget, no extra time.

  1. 01.

    A context base first.

    Before any automation, I built the project's knowledge base: scope of work, plus transcripts from every meeting type (dailies and refinements with the dev team, client calls, walkthrough sessions with the designer). Context is KING. Without it, every automation is a toy.

  2. 02.

    Daily notes on autopilot.

    Our early dailies were dense: half standup, half refinement, often splitting into subgroups to work through the build in detail. I built a prompt that turns transcripts into structured notes (who does what, next actions) and posts them as a draft straight to the Slack channel. I review and send. Nothing gets lost, and in a project with this much moving, things used to get lost weekly. Over 40 daily notes produced this way and counting.

  3. 03.

    Client follow-ups that write themselves.

    Read.ai records the call, Claude drafts the summary email in Gmail. After every client meeting, the follow-up is waiting for me instead of hanging over me. 16 client status summaries and counting.

  4. 04.

    A requirements engine.

    The designer walked the client through the system screen by screen; those recorded sessions became my raw material. I iterated a prompt that turns them into functional specifications, and this was the hard part: AI loves generating volume nobody reads. It took many iterations to get essence instead of noise. The prompt also flags assumptions, edge cases, and open questions automatically, so the client gets pinged at the requirements stage, not mid-development. A spec that would take hours to days manually now takes minutes to generate and 30 to 60 minutes to verify. 17 specifications written since April.

  5. 05.

    Designing the admin panel from the ground up.

    For the admin panel, I fed the knowledge base every existing specification plus the contract and client conversations, and asked Claude to propose what the panel should do and which features it needed. Crucially, I had it mark which features fell outside the scope of work and prioritize the rest (P0, P1, P2). That output drove the client conversations about the panel and became the basis for the designer's clickable prototype.

  6. 06.

    C-level reporting without the Friday dread.

    Weekly board updates in a fixed format used to take me up to an hour, at 4 PM on a Friday. Now Claude reads the daily notes channel and the client summaries and drafts the update in the required format, waiting on the right Slack channel. I verify in about 15 minutes.

HOW I DECIDED WHAT TO AUTOMATE

My criteria were simple: repetitive, text-heavy, and expensive when dropped. Documentation and reporting scored highest, so they went first. What stayed deliberately human: every decision, every client conversation, and the verification of every single AI output. That's the part nobody puts on LinkedIn. We can generate a million pages in minutes, but verification is the real work now, and it takes real time. AI drafts. I sign.

THE CATCH THAT PAID FOR ITSELF

The clearest proof of why verification and requirements work matters: while mapping order, visit, payment, and payout statuses into a single cross-level consistency table, the prompt surfaced that there was no distinction between a visit taking place and the carer being paid. As written, the backend would have released payouts the moment a visit ended, removing the 48-hour grace period needed to catch a no-show or dispute before money left the platform.

We introduced a separate "Finished" state (payout Processing, 48h hold) versus "Completed" (payout Paid), and confirmed "In Progress" belongs only at the visit level, not the order level, before any of it reached development. A logic gap caught on paper instead of in production, with real money on the line.

THE RESULTS

  • ✳Weekly C-level status: from up to 60 minutes to about 15 minutes of review
  • ✳Functional specification: from hours or days of manual writing to minutes of generation plus 30 to 60 minutes of verification
  • ✳17 specifications, 16 client status summaries, and 40+ daily notes produced since April
  • ✳Edge cases and open questions surfaced at the requirements stage, before development, not during it

the honest part

WHAT I LEARNED

  • ✳Context is KING; automations without a knowledge base are demos, not systems.
  • ✳Verification is the bottleneck nobody talks about.
  • ✳Building a working system takes time, and there is no universal AI workflow yet; most companies have no rules for this at all, so you test, fail, and iterate.
  • ✳Brain fry is real: more generated content means more decisions and faster burnout if you don't manage it.
  • ✳And the biggest shift: as AI speeds up development, the bottleneck moves to knowing what to build and for whom. Requirements are becoming more important, not less.

WHAT'S NEXT

I'm currently testing spec-driven development, where specifications drive work on real repositories. The system I built was version one. I'm more interested in what version two looks like.
